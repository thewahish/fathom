Bayes, Seen as Area

A test being "90% accurate" sounds like it settles things. It doesn't — not until you know how rare what it's testing for is. Drag the population apart and watch why.

90%
90%
8%

The square is everyone. The amber vertical line splits it into who actually has the condition (violet tint, left) and who doesn't (right) — drag it to change how rare the condition is. Each amber horizontal line splits its column into who tests positive (blue tint, top) and who tests negative (bottom) — the left one is how well the test catches true cases, the right one is how well it clears healthy people. Once you get a positive result, only the blue region matters, and it has two very different sources: violet-and-blue is a true positive, blue-alone is a false alarm. The posterior — the number in the last box — is just the true positives' share of all the blue. Start on "rare disease": a 90%-accurate test still gives you only ~8% odds of actually having it. Now click "99%-accurate test" — barely moves. Then "50/50 prior" — suddenly the same test is a near-lock. The test never changed. The population it's aimed at did.
